# Interview Guide

This is the question bank and challenge protocol for the research-proposal-interview skill. Consult per-section as the interview proceeds.

## The balanced challenge protocol

Apply this after every substantive answer the user gives. It's the spine of the skill.

1. **Name the implicit assumption.** Reflect back the unstated belief their answer rests on. ("It sounds like you're assuming X is stable across regimes — is that right?")
2. **Ask for evidence.** What's the basis for that assumption? Lived experience, a paper, a prior experiment, intuition? All are valid answers; the goal is to make the basis explicit.
3. **Propose one falsifier or alternative.** Offer a single concrete way the assumption could be wrong, or a single alternative explanation for what they expect to see. Not three, not a list — one well-chosen one.
4. **Accept the response and update the doc.** If they have a good rebuttal, write it into the doc as part of the assumption's defense. If they don't, mark the assumption as a known weak point and move on. Do not re-litigate.

The tone is "thoughtful committee member," not "hostile reviewer." The user should feel sharpened, not attacked.

## Section-by-section questions

### Section 1 — Question & Significance

**Primary question:**
- "What's the single end-question this research needs to answer? State it as a question, not a topic."

**Follow-ups (pick what's missing):**
- "Who's the audience for the answer — yourself, your team, a journal, a funder, a customer?"
- "What changes the day you have a credible answer? What can you do that you can't do now?"
- "What's your current best guess at the answer, before any new work?"

**Challenge prompts:**
- If the question is too broad: "That's a research *area*, not a question. What's the specific decision or claim that an answer would resolve?"
- If the user has no prior: "An honest 'I don't know' is fine, but priors are usually richer than that. What would surprise you?"

### Section 2 — Success Criteria

**Primary question:**
- "Suppose you've done the work. What evidence would convince *you* the answer is yes? What would convince you it's no?"

**Follow-ups:**
- "What does an inconclusive result look like, and what would you do about it?"
- "Is there a threshold — effect size, accuracy, p-value, qualitative pattern — that you'd commit to in advance?"

**Challenge prompts:**
- If success criteria are vague: "If we ran this and got [plausible result], would you call that a success? Why or why not?"
- If criteria are post-hoc-able: "Could you commit to those criteria *before* seeing data? If not, let's tighten them now."

### Section 3 — Prior Work & Gap

**Primary question:**
- "Who else has worked on this or something adjacent, and where did they leave off?"

**Follow-ups:**
- "What's the strongest existing answer to your question, even if you disagree with it?"
- "What's the gap you're filling — a missing dataset, a missing method, a missing question, or a missing synthesis?"

**Challenge prompt:**
- "What's the steel-man version of the position that this research isn't needed because the answer is already known?"

### Section 4 — Theory of the Problem

**Primary question:**
- "What's your mental model of why this question is tractable *now*? What mechanism do you think is at play?"

**Follow-ups:**
- "If your theory is right, what else should we observe that we haven't looked for?"
- "What's the simplest version of your theory you'd be willing to defend?"

**Challenge prompts:**
- "What's an alternative theory that would predict the same things you expect to see?"
- "What assumption is doing the most work here? If it broke, what would still survive?"

### Section 5 — Lines of Attack

**Primary question:**
- "What are 2–4 distinct strategies that could each, on their own, plausibly answer the question?"

**Follow-ups:**
- "Rank them by your current credence that they'd work."
- "For your top-ranked one, what would have to be true for it to succeed?"
- "Are any of these mutually exclusive, or could you pursue them in parallel?"

**Challenge prompts:**
- If only one strategy is offered: "What's a strategy you've considered and rejected, and why? Sometimes the rejected ones are worth a second look."
- If strategies aren't really distinct: "These two seem like variants of the same approach. What's a strategy that uses fundamentally different evidence or method?"

### Section 6 — Immediate Experiment(s)

**Primary question:**
- "What's the single next experiment that would most reduce uncertainty about your root question?"

**Follow-ups:**
- "What's the hypothesis, in one sentence?"
- "What's the primary metric, and what threshold matters?"
- "Before you start, what will you have decided about each plausible outcome?"

**Challenge prompts (the adversarial trio):**
- "What test, if it failed, would actually kill this hypothesis? Not just dent it — kill it."
- "What's an alternative explanation that would produce the same expected result, and how would you distinguish?"
- "If you got exactly the result you expect, would a skeptic be convinced? What would they ask for?"

### Section 7 — Conditional Next Steps

**Primary question:**
- "Sketch — don't commit — what you'd do in the three most likely outcome regimes of your immediate experiment."

**Follow-ups:**
- "Which conditional branch are you most worried about being unprepared for?"
- "Is there an outcome where the right move is to abandon this direction entirely? What would that look like?"

**Challenge prompt:**
- "If your immediate experiment is null, what's the difference between 'we got the answer (it's no)' and 'our setup couldn't have detected the effect'? How would you tell them apart?"

### Section 8 — Risks, Kill Criteria, Assumptions

**Primary question:**
- "Under what observations would you abandon this entire line of inquiry?"

**Follow-ups:**
- "What's the most dangerous assumption — the one that, if wrong, invalidates the most other work?"
- "What's a risk you're aware of but don't have a mitigation for?"

**Challenge prompt:**
- "If you ran this project for a year and produced no clear answer, what would you bet was the cause? Let's plan against that now."

*(After this section, trigger the pre-mortem agent — see exploration.md.)*

### Section 9 — Speculative Horizon

**Primary question:**
- "If everything worked, what's the deliverable — the paper, the system, the decision you'd make?"

**Follow-ups:**
- "What's the smallest version of that deliverable that would still be valuable?"
- "Is there an interim deliverable at, say, the halfway point that would be worth shipping on its own?"

**Challenge prompt:**
- "What's the version of success that would be technically real but practically useless? How would you avoid landing there?"

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Multi-question turns.** One question, wait, respond, move on. The skill loses its character if it becomes a form.
- **Stacking challenges.** One challenge per answer. A second challenge becomes interrogation.
- **Filling in the doc with the user's exact words verbatim.** Synthesize. The doc should be tighter and more structured than the conversation.
- **Letting "speculative" answers be tagged "committed."** Push back on overconfidence in the tags.
- **Skipping the doc update.** If you've gone three turns without editing the file, stop and write before asking the next question.
